## 1. CONTRARIAN — We stopped selling software. Now we sell automation power to employees.

```
Everyone sells software to hospitals. We stopped.

We built a 100-bed hospital HIS, charged ₹2–3 lakhs, struggled for 18 months watching it sit in a drawer. IT teams didn't own it. Workflows didn't change. ROI was invisible.

So we asked a billing clerk what she actually needed.

Not a system. Not a dashboard. Automation power. A markdown file with her SOP, a tiny Agent Factory UI, and permission to map her own daily friction (approval chains, duplicate entry, manual reconciliation) into a bot that runs at 5am.

She owned it. She iterated it. Within 3 weeks, she cut her reconciliation time by 4 hours a week.

Now every employee is an automation builder. We sell the platform, not the solution. Hospitals get 20+ automations running bottom-up instead of one massive system gathering dust.

Everyone's still selling software. We're selling the power to automate your own chaos.
```

## 2. FRAMEWORK — How a 3-agent pilot prevented $200K in scope creep

```
**Three Agents, One Department, No Creep**

We scoped an AI pilot to GDMO's HR function — exactly 3 agents, no expansion roadmap, no "future departments" language. Felt restrictive. Prevented $200K in scope creep.

Without hard boundaries, these projects leak. Finance sees what HR got, wants it. Operations lists workflows. Suddenly you're integrating five departments with five different data models, compliance frameworks, and approval cycles.

The rule: **Pilot one function end-to-end before you expand.** Not because scale is impossible — because you need to know what actually scales. One department shows you real integration cost, real data quality, real approval overhead. Extrapolate to five departments and you see the $200K leak before it happens.

We locked HR, locked agent count, locked model choice to "TBD post-pilot." Finance waits. Operations prioritizes later.

Scope creep reads as ambition. It's usually just math you haven't done.
```

## 3. DATA-POINT — Why your Bill of Quantities can't be verified (and costs you deals)

```
Two proposals in two weeks. Both killed in BOQ review.

Client opens the spreadsheet, doesn't recognize the calculation path, sends it back in 48 hours: "We can't verify this."

We dug into why. The SMDB-to-DB cable quantities were 40% off. Turns out they were copy-pasted from another project—not recalculated for these drawings.

So here's the pattern: opaque BOQ → client can't verify → doesn't trust the price → deal stalls.

We rebuilt from scratch, floor-by-floor breakdown using their specified methodology. Longer upfront. But the next 3 proposals closed. No pushback on numbers.

Transparency in B2B contracts isn't optional. It's the permission to move forward.
```
