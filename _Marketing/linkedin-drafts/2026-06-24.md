## 1. ANECDOTE — Our form routing broke across 10+ workflows. One JotForm CC setting fixed it—saving 5 hours/month in manual distribution.

```
Our form routing was a nightmare. Ten-plus workflows firing off submissions to different inboxes, different people, different vendors. No one notified consistently. No one got their pre-filled links.

The real friction: someone had to manually copy each form URL and email it to the right person. Every. Single. Time.

We were doing manual logistics for a system that should be automatic.

Then we noticed JotForm's CC field existed. We'd never used it.

Added one email address to the CC settings. Submissions now land in a central inbox. The app reads them, extracts the pre-filled link, surfaces it directly to the user. No human in the loop.

Five hours a month gone.

Fourteen thousand old form links still on the old system. We're still deciding whether to migrate those. Probably not worth it.

But every new workflow gets the CC fix now. It's automatic.

The lesson stuck: the tool usually has the feature. You're just not using it. You're paying the cost of your own blindness.
```

## 2. FRAMEWORK — Slack channels fragment instantly. One master 'Pulse of Project' document became our heartbeat instead.

```
Slack channels fragment. Video links die. Decisions live in thread archaeology. Six months later, your team is relitigating vendor choices because nobody remembers why you made them.

Our rule: one document is your project's heartbeat. Blockers, decisions, next moves, meeting recordings—everything lives there. Drive folder for videos (searchable, persistent, no Slack rot). Single source of truth.

Our project lead owns it. Meeting prep dropped 60%. We killed the rehashing.

Catch: it only works if people actually write in it. Most teams build the doc and ghost it. If your team won't spend five minutes a week updating, you still lose.
```

## 3. CONTRARIAN — We didn't guess at report categories. One client conversation shaped all five of them.

```
Most teams build report category systems by guessing what matters, then iterate when clinic operators push back. We skipped the guessing.

Spent an hour with one client — BT, runs a busy clinic — asking what actually kills productivity when you're processing 200 EEG reports a week. Not theoretical categories. Just friction: which decisions slow her down, where context switching eats time, what she'd pay to avoid.

That conversation became five categories. Shipped it. Zero change requests since.

Compare that to the standard arc: ship 10 categories, harvest feedback for 8 more, realize you've muddied the model, rebuild it twice, ship a cleaner version nine months later.

One operator's workflow beats ten feature-voting cycles.

When you're building for operators, operator time is cheaper than engineer time. Spend it. Depth over breadth.
```
