## 1. CONTRARIAN — We capped doctor summaries to 8 next steps — because more than that and nothing actually gets done

```
We capped doctor summaries at 8 next steps.

Not because of some UX principle. Because I watched what actually happened when we gave more.

12 action items in a post-consult summary. Doctor reads the first three, skims the rest, closes the tab. Patient goes home with a PDF that looks thorough and changes nothing.

The summary felt complete. The care wasn't.

So when we built the patient report flow for Dr. Shweta's clinic, I made a hard call — the system will not generate more than 8 next steps. Ever. Doesn't matter how complex the case.

If your AI can produce 14 action items, congratulations. You've built a very sophisticated way to overwhelm a doctor who's already seen 30 patients today.

The constraint forces the model to prioritise. Forces us to ask: if this patient acts on only 3 of these, which 3 matter most?

That's not a limitation. That's the actual clinical thinking we're trying to support.

Most people building health AI are optimising for completeness. We're optimising for what gets done.

There's a difference. A large one.
```

## 2. FRAMEWORK — A tiered reschedule policy that keeps early patients free but charges after 3 months — the specific rule that protects slot utilization without punishing loyal patients

```
3 months.

That's the cutoff.

If a patient reschedules within 3 months of their last visit — it's free. No friction, no charge. They're still in the care cycle.

Beyond 3 months? Paid consultation. With a 50% discount, because they're not new — but they're not active either.

This one rule does three things at once:

Protects slot utilization. A rescheduled slot that keeps getting pushed costs the clinic real money. 24 slots a month isn't infinite.

Doesn't punish loyal patients. Charging someone who came 10 weeks ago the same as a walk-in is bad medicine and bad business.

Creates a natural re-engagement trigger. The 50% discount makes returning easy. It's not a wall — it's a ramp.

We're building this into the doctors-digital-office admin layer. Clinic admin sets the policy once. The system applies it automatically at booking.

No one has to remember the rule. No one has to have an awkward conversation at the front desk.

The friction was always in enforcement, not the policy itself.

Most clinics have a reschedule policy in someone's head. That's not a policy — that's a liability.
```

## 3. ANECDOTE — Building a first-line triage layer between patients and the senior doctor — the operational decision that made 24 slots/month actually scale

```
24 slots a month sounds like nothing.

For a senior psychiatrist running complex cases, it's actually the ceiling. You can't just add more slots — the quality collapses.

So the question wasn't "how do we book more patients."

It was: who should actually be reaching Dr. Shweta?

We built a first-line layer. Ridhima sits before the slot. She screens, orients, and sorts. Only patients who are genuinely ready — with context prepared, reports reviewed, next steps pre-mapped — land in front of the doctor.

The protocol was specific:
Within 3 months of last visit → free follow-up slot.
Beyond 3 months → paid consultation, 50% discount applies.
Every summary handed to Dr. Shweta has exactly 8 next steps. Not 12. Not "as discussed." Eight.

What happened to the 24 slots? They started doing the work of 40.

Not because we added hours. Because we stopped letting the senior doctor be the intake desk.

This is the actual problem with scaling clinical practices in India. Everyone talks about "digitising" — they mean making a PDF look nicer. The real work is deciding who touches what, in what order, with what information already resolved.

The software we're building around this (doctors-digital-office) is just encoding that decision tree. The decision came first.

Most clinics are bottlenecked at the top because no one has formally defined what "prepared to see the doctor" even means.
```
